# Storyline Architecture Prompt

Goal: turn a proposed book, source corpus, audience, and intended impact into a storyline map that can guide the whole manuscript.

Produce these refs:

- premise and reader promise
- audience model and reading situation
- reader-style contract that defines who the book is for and what expression will feel natural to those readers
- author/source stance map for major cases and examples, including whether the author team is an outside observer, participant-observer, project designer, project executor, evaluator, or advocate
- central argument or narrative engine
- chapter thesis chain with escalation logic
- chapter function contract with each chapter's single job, new argument movement, adjacent-chapter handoffs, and claims/sections it should avoid repeating
- early concept map for the book's recurring terms, including where each term is first defined or foreshadowed before readers need it
- whole-book core model map naming two or three reusable models, their first appearance, later applications, figure/table candidates, and final recovery points
- case evidence ladder for major cases and examples, separating constructed scenes, typical scenarios, documented process evidence, authorized interviews/materials, and outcome/impact evidence
- reviewer/owner critique absorption map when critique exists, showing which suggestions update storyline, chapter jobs, evidence gaps, core models, style rules, or owner decisions
- evidence map with source coverage and known gaps
- tone and style contract
- risks, open questions, and owner decisions

Before drafting or approving chapter work, determine the book's reader-facing identity: primary readers, secondary readers, excluded readers, reading situation, reader anxieties, expected prior knowledge, and what prose will feel natural rather than generic. Keep the priority explicit: primary readers define the writing target; secondary readers only add compatible accessibility constraints unless the owner explicitly makes them co-primary readers. Do not let read-along secondary readers create separate chapter obligations, explanatory detours, lower-density exposition, difficulty downgrades, or a competing voice target. If the owner did not specify these and they cannot be inferred with high confidence from the sources, return a typed owner-question blocker instead of silently choosing a generic audience or style.

Before chapter drafting, determine the stance of major cases. When the owner or sources show that the author team designed, executed, or directly participated in a case, record that as a practice-involved case in the reader-style contract and evidence map. The later chapter should use active design language such as "we anticipated..." or the book's approved equivalent when natural, while keeping unsupported outcomes as evidence gaps. Do not default practice-involved cases to third-party public-source observation.

For high-quality nonfiction, make chapter function explicit before body drafting. A chapter can reinforce the book's central premise, but it must add a distinguishable movement: cause, boundary, concept definition, field mechanism, capability model, institutional practice, policy implication, or value-level close. If two adjacent chapters share the same job, merge, reorder, or narrow them before drafting. For endings, distinguish institutional/system summary from value or mission-level conclusion instead of letting the final chapter and conclusion perform the same role.

Establish the reader's concept map early. If a term will carry several chapters of argument, do not wait until the middle of the book to define it without an earlier orientation. Add a preface or first-chapter preview when needed, then let the full chapter definition arrive later with examples and cases.

Turn tables and figures into core models, not only information lists. Select two or three models that the whole book can reuse. Each model should have a purpose, a figure/table candidate, an introduction chapter, at least one later application chapter, and a final synthesis point.

Separate case evidence levels before writing. Constructed scenes can explain a typical problem; documented process cases can support operating claims; authorized interviews/materials can support participant claims; outcome evidence can support impact claims. When only a lower evidence level exists, the chapter may still use the case, but it must keep the claim at that level or return a typed evidence gap such as a real-case blocker.

When a reviewer or owner critique names structural issues such as repetition, low case density, weak modelization, unclear concepts, or publication-level monotony, convert those points into storyline refs first. A critique is absorbed only when it updates chapter function contracts, concept maps, case evidence ladders, core model maps, style rules, or quality gates; a prose-only cleanup is insufficient for systemic critique.

Use affirmative prose as the default style. Prefer direct claims, concrete verbs, and active transitions. Rewrite vague contrast formulas into positive commitments. Track phrases that sound generic, inflated, or evasive and return a revision note with concrete replacements.

Close with explicit storyline-map, chapter-plan, reader-style-contract, style-contract, owner-handoff, or typed-blocker refs. Generated surface readiness, provider completion, and suite pass remain evidence only.
